Asymmetric Roll Pass – Method & Results

PyRoll GUI  |  Date: May 2026

1. What is an Asymmetric Roll Pass?

An Asymmetric Roll Pass (AsymmetricRollPass) uses two rolls with different groove geometries (upper and lower). A typical example is the asymmetric entry pass of a rolling line where one side has a shaped groove (e.g. SplineGroove) and the other side is flat (FlatGroove). The asymmetry produces an unsymmetric cross-section and an offset neutral axis.

PyRolL Core does not natively support asymmetric roll passes. The PyRoll GUI implements a dedicated simulation strategy for this case, described in the following sections.

2. Simulation Strategy

2.1 Two Symmetric Sub-Passes

The asymmetric pass is decomposed into two independent symmetric TwoRollPasses:

  • Upper sub-pass: both rolls use the upper groove geometry.
  • Lower sub-pass: both rolls use the lower groove geometry.

Both sub-passes share the same gap, nominal roll radius, velocity and inlet profile. Each is solved independently by the PyRolL simulation kernel. The sub-passes are shown as separate entries in the results table (flagged internally as is_sub_pass: true) for transparency.

2.2 Averaged Process Parameters

All scalar process parameters for the combined AsymmetricRollPass result are computed as the arithmetic mean of the two sub-passes:

2.3 Geometric Cross-Section: Lendl Pillar Method

The averaged scalars cannot describe the shape of the asymmetric exit cross-section. For this, the Lendl pillar method is used.

ℹ The LendlAnalyser is fully embedded in the PyRoll GUI backend — it does not depend on the optional pyroll-pillar-model plugin.

3. Lendl Pillar Analysis

3.1 Pillar Discretisation

The inlet cross-section is discretised into \(N = 50\) vertical pillars of equal width \(\Delta x\) spanning the half-width of the profile. Each pillar \(i\) has:

\[ x_i = \left(i - \tfrac{1}{2}\right) \Delta x, \quad \Delta x = \frac{x_{\max}}{N} \]

The inlet height of each pillar is read from the cross-section polygon by intersecting a vertical line at \(x_i\) with the profile boundary.

3.2 Roll Gap Height per Pillar

The gap height between the upper and lower roll contour at pillar position \(x_i\) is computed from the groove contour lines. Pillars are flagged as in contact if their inlet height exceeds the local roll gap height.

\[ h_{\text{gap},i} = y_{\text{upper}}(x_i) - y_{\text{lower}}(x_i) \] \[ \text{contact}_i = h_{\text{in},i} > h_{\text{gap},i} \]

3.3 Spreading (Pawelski 2000)

The lateral spread of each pillar is computed according to Pawelski (2000), using the equivalent width-to-height ratio of the entire cross-section to select the coefficient set:

\[ \lambda_{\text{spread},i} = 1 + b_1 \left(\frac{\Delta h_i}{h_{eq}}\right)^{b_2} \left(\frac{l_{c,i}}{w_{eq}}\right)^{b_3} \left(\frac{h_{eq}}{w_{eq}}\right)^{b_4} \]

where \(\Delta h_i\) is the draught of pillar \(i\), \(l_{c,i}\) is its contact length, and \(h_{eq}\), \(w_{eq}\) are the equivalent height and width of the inlet cross-section. The coefficients \(b_1 \ldots b_4\) depend on the width-to-height ratio:

Ratio \(w/h\)\(b_1\)\(b_2\)\(b_3\)\(b_4\)
\(w/h < 1\)1.2401.6260.788−0.056
\(w/h = 1\)0.3120.8660.89219.946
\(w/h > 1\)0.4961.0251.1421.166

3.4 Contact Length per Pillar

\[ l_{c,i} = \sqrt{\frac{r_u \cdot r_l}{r_u + r_l} \cdot \Delta h_i} \]

where \(r_u\), \(r_l\) are the local roll radii derived from the nominal radius and pillar draught.

3.5 Output Cross-Section Reconstruction

The output pillar widths are \(w_{\text{out},i} = w_{\text{in},i} \cdot \lambda_{\text{spread},i}\). The output cross-section polygon is built by:

  • computing cumulative output pillar boundaries from the centre outward;
  • reading the upper and lower y-coordinates of the exit groove contours at the new pillar positions;
  • assembling a closed polygon from upper-right → upper-left → lower-left → lower-right vertices, mirrored about the y-axis for the left half.

The resulting polygon represents the asymmetric exit cross-section in the pass coordinate frame (x = spread direction, y = closing direction).

3.6 Symmetric Sub-Analyses

In addition to the combined asymmetric analysis, two symmetric sub-analyses are run with the same Lendl method:

  • Upper-symmetric: upper groove on both sides.
  • Lower-symmetric: lower groove on both sides.

These are hypothetical analyses (the gap geometry differs from the true asymmetric pass) that isolate the contribution of each groove to the spreading behaviour. Their output cross-sections and per-pillar spreading/elongation curves are shown in the Pillar-Fill Plot and Pillar-Chart for qualitative comparison.

4. Coordinate Frame and Orientation

The AsymmetricRollPass is typically used as a vertical pass (rolls arranged horizontally, closing direction = horizontal = x-axis in world frame). An _orientation_delta value (in degrees) tracks the total rotation applied to the inlet profile relative to the global horizontal frame.

5. Results Overview

5.1 Combined AsymmetricRollPass Result

The main result entry (type = AsymmetricRollPass) contains:

  • Geometry: in/out height, width, cross-section area, reduction — from the Lendl exit cross-section in world frame. (Lendl)
  • Process scalars: force, torque, power, velocity, RPM, bite angle, filling ratio — arithmetic mean of both sub-passes. (averaged)
  • Temperatures: exit mean, core, surface temperature — averaged from both sub-passes. (averaged)
  • Strain / flow stress: averaged from both sub-passes. (averaged)
  • Profile contours: in_profile_contour (inlet polygon), out_profile_contour (Lendl exit polygon). (Lendl)
  • Roll contours: upper and lower groove contour lines, rotated to technological display frame.
  • Pillar data: full pillar arrays for front-end visualisation (see Section 6).
  • Per-roll values: force_upper, force_lower, torque_upper, torque_lower, velocity_upper, velocity_lower (stored individually for transparency).

5.2 Sub-Pass Results

The two symmetric sub-pass results (is_sub_pass: true) are stored as separate entries in the pass list and contain the full PyRolL output for each symmetric simulation (geometry, temperatures, force, torque, contours, ring temperatures). They are used for the Roll Pass Visualization of each sub-pass.

6. Visualisation Plots

6.1 Roll Pass Visualization

Shown in the Pass Design tab. Displays the upper and lower groove contour lines with the inlet and outlet profile overlaid. For an AsymmetricRollPass the upper and lower roll contours differ visibly.

6.2 Roll Pass Result Plot (Pass Plots / Overall Plots tabs)

Shows the combined result: roll contours (black), inlet profile (blue) and exit profile (red) in the technological display frame. The asymmetric exit shape produced by the Lendl analyser is clearly visible — one side follows the shaped groove, the other side is flatter or differently contoured.

6.3 Pillar Fill Plot

Two panels side by side:

  • Left – Symmetric Sub-Analyses: the upper half of the upper-symmetric exit cross-section and the lower half of the lower-symmetric exit cross-section, overlaid on the inlet profile. Qualitatively shows the different spreading contribution from each groove.
  • Right – Combined Lendl (Asymmetric): inlet cross-section (blue outline) and the combined asymmetric exit cross-section with the lateral spreading areas highlighted (yellow fill). Groove contour lines are shown clipped to the pillar x-range.

6.4 Pillar Chart (Elongation & Spreading)

Per-pillar charts showing:

  • Spreading factor \(\lambda_{spread}\) vs. pillar position — for upper-sym, lower-sym, and combined.
  • Elongation factor \(\lambda_{elong}\) vs. pillar position — same three curves.

Pillars not in contact (draught = 0) have \(\lambda_{spread} = 1\) and \(\lambda_{elong} = 1\). The asymmetry between upper and lower groove is reflected in the different spreading profiles of the two sub-analyses.

6.5 Out Profile Plot (Pass Design tab)

Shows the Lendl exit cross-section after the last pass of each sequence. For a sequence following an AsymmetricRollPass, the profile is automatically x-mirrored in the display to match the technological orientation convention (curved side on the left).

6.6 3D Cross-Section Evolution Plot

Displays the cross-section evolution along the pass sequence in 3D. For the AsymmetricRollPass the x-coordinates of the displayed cross-section are negated (mirror in x) so that the curved side appears consistently on the left in all sequences. The raw Lendl geometry is stored in world frame; the 3D plot applies this mirror internally.

7. Multi-Sequence Workflow

When an AsymmetricRollPass is the last pass of a sequence, its exit cross-section (out_profile_cross_section_coords) is automatically carried forward as the inlet profile for the next sequence. The coordinate transformation applied:

  • Lendl output is in pass frame.
  • Rotated by \(-\delta\) (negative orientation_delta) → world frame.
  • Stored as out_profile_cross_section_coords and fed to the next sequence via helpers.create_initial_profile().

In the next sequence all pass result plots apply an automatic x-mirror correction (display_mirror_x = true) so that 2D contour displays show the correct left/right orientation.

8. Approximation Notes

⚠ Symmetric sub-pass approximation: Each sub-pass is simulated with the same groove on both rolls. The true asymmetric boundary condition (different upper and lower groove contact simultaneously) is not solved by PyRolL Core. The averaged scalars are therefore an approximation — they are most accurate when the two grooves have similar depths and contact geometries.
⚠ Lendl geometry approximation: The Lendl pillar model assumes independent lateral spreading of each vertical strip. Longitudinal coupling between adjacent pillars (e.g. near groove flanks) is not modelled. For highly asymmetric grooves or large lateral spread gradients the predicted cross-section shape may deviate from the real geometry.
